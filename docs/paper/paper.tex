%% ===================== ABSTRACT =====================
\begin{abstract}
We present a three-stage hierarchical soft-fusion framework for cryptocurrency Buy/Hold/Sell signal classification on Bitcoin (BTC) and Ethereum (ETH) daily series. The framework decomposes the trading decision into three pattern-recognition sub-problems: a \emph{trend classifier} (Stage~1) that estimates the local price-trend regime from technical indicators using a ZigZag-based offline label, a \emph{macroeconomic regime classifier} (Stage~2) that produces Bull/Neutral/Bear labels through a deterministic finite-state machine over VIX, S\&P 500, yield-curve, DXY and M2 signals, and a \emph{signal classifier} (Stage~3) that fuses the two upstream posterior probability vectors with six oscillator features. Four classifiers (XGBoost, LightGBM, Random Forest, MLP) are tuned per stage with Optuna and 5-fold inner-CV walk-forward, then evaluated on outer expanding-window walk-forward out-of-fold (OOF) predictions covering 2017--2025 for BTC (3{,}200 days) and 2020--2025 for ETH (2{,}000 days). On BTC the full 3-stage architecture attains a Sharpe ratio of $1.15$ versus $0.95$ for Buy~\&~Hold, with a $40\%$ drawdown reduction. On ETH a flat 6-oscillator baseline is preferred (Sharpe $0.52$) while every architecture outperforms the loss-making Buy~\&~Hold ($-7\%$). Our principal empirical finding is that the optimal architectural depth is asset-specific and cannot be predicted from frame-level metrics alone: macro-F1 stays in the 0.34--0.37 band across configurations, yet Hold-aware trading rules transform weak class probabilities into economically meaningful trades.
\end{abstract}

Hierarchical classification, cryptocurrency, trading signals, walk-forward cross-validation, soft fusion, ablation study, gradient boosting, regime detection.

%% ===================== 1. INTRODUCTION =====================
\section{Introduction}
Cryptocurrency markets are noisy, non-stationary, and respond to a mixture of micro-structure, sentiment, and macroeconomic shocks. Supervised approaches that map raw price features directly to a Buy/Hold/Sell label tend to learn idiosyncratic relationships that fail to generalise across regimes \cite{sezer2020,patel2015trend}. Decomposing the decision \emph{hierarchically}---first identifying the short-term trend, then the macro context, and finally the trade---offers three benefits: (i)~each sub-problem has a clearer signal-to-noise ratio; (ii)~intermediate posteriors are inspectable and auditable; and (iii)~the modular pipeline can be ablated to isolate where predictive value resides.

This work formulates and empirically evaluates such a three-stage classifier on BTC and ETH daily data. The pipeline uses \emph{soft fusion}: the Stage-3 classifier receives the full Stage-1 trend posterior vector $\hat p_{\mathrm{trend}}$ and the Stage-2 regime label $\hat p_{\mathrm{regime}}$ as additional input features, in addition to the standard oscillator inputs (Fig.~\ref{fig:arch_diagram} illustrates the full configuration plus three reduced ablations). We compare four canonical pattern-recognition algorithms (XGBoost, LightGBM, Random Forest, MLP) at every stage, perform a four-architecture ablation (Flat, 2-Stage Trend, 2-Stage Macro, 3-Stage Full) and a three-trading-rule study, and assess the system on both classification (Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1, ROC-AUC) and economic (Sharpe, drawdown, return) metrics.

\subsection{Related Work}
\textbf{Hierarchical and cascade classifiers.} The idea of decomposing a hard decision into a sequence of easier sub-problems has a long history: hierarchical mixtures of experts \cite{jordan1994hme}, stacked generalisation \cite{wolpert1992stacked}, and the hierarchical-classification survey of Silla and Freitas \cite{silla2011hierarchical}. In financial time series, N-HiTS \cite{challu2023nhits} addressed multi-horizon forecasting and Crossformer \cite{zhang2023crossformer} multivariate dependencies, while Zou \emph{et al.} \cite{zou2024cascadelstm} reported Sharpe gains by placing a cascaded LSTM upstream of an RL trader. For tabular daily data of the size encountered here, gradient-boosted trees still tend to outperform deep architectures \cite{grinsztajn2022tabular,shwartzziv2022tabular}, motivating our choice of XGBoost/LightGBM/Random Forest alongside an MLP.

\textbf{Crypto signal classification, regime detection and trading-rule design.} Crypto-specific approaches have polarised between monolithic deep models (e.g.~LSTM directional predictors \cite{patel2015fusion,sezer2018cnn}) and rule-based technical strategies. The confidence-threshold cascade of Kuznetsov \emph{et al.} \cite{kuznetsov2025confidence} is the closest prior art to our Hold-aware Stage~3, achieving $82.7\%$ directional accuracy at only $12.0\%$ market coverage---an explicit acknowledgement that abstention is more valuable than forced trades. Macroeconomic regime detection has historically relied on Markov-switching \cite{hamilton1989markov} and rule-based filters over VIX and yield-curve indicators \cite{ang2002regime,andersen2003realized}; we adopt a deterministic finite-state machine in the same spirit (Section~\ref{sec:fsm}). Methodologically, we follow Lopez de Prado \cite{lopez2018amlfinance,lopez2018tenreasons} in using expanding-window walk-forward CV with embargo gaps to avoid leakage, and evaluate stateful, defensive and probability-weighted trading rules in the spirit of Bailey and Lopez de Prado \cite{bailey2014deflated}.

\textbf{Limitations of prior work and positioning.} Two gaps motivate this study. First, hierarchical financial-ML pipelines have been compared mostly at the \emph{flat}-vs-\emph{full} extremes \cite{zou2024cascadelstm}; few works perform a controlled depth ablation that adds upstream stages one at a time, making it hard to attribute observed gains to a specific stage. Second, prior crypto-signal studies typically report either frame-level metrics (Accuracy, F1) or economic metrics (Sharpe, return) but rarely their joint behaviour, concealing the case where a low-F1 model is in fact a useful trading agent because the Hold class lets it abstain. We address both: a four-architecture depth ablation per asset, and side-by-side classification and economic metrics on identical OOF predictions.

\subsection{Contributions}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{A three-stage hierarchical soft-fusion framework} for crypto trading-signal classification: a ZigZag-labelled trend classifier at Stage~1, a rule-based macro finite-state machine at Stage~2, and a 4-classifier signal head at Stage~3 fed with the upstream posteriors.
\item \textbf{An asset-specific architecture finding}: the 3-Stage Full pipeline is empirically best for BTC (Sharpe $1.15$, $+21\%$ over Buy~\&~Hold) while the \emph{flat} 6-oscillator baseline is best for ETH (Sharpe $0.52$ vs.~$0.34$ for 3-Stage Full). Hierarchical fusion adds value on long, regime-rich histories but overfits a shorter one---a concrete instance of no-free-lunch in financial ML.
\item \textbf{Hold-aware trading rules that turn weak class probabilities into Sharpe-positive strategies}: although Stage-3 macro-F1 sits at $0.37$ and ROC-AUC at $0.53$ (only marginally above chance), stateful and probability-weighted rules that abstain on uncertain days deliver Sharpe~$1.15$ on BTC and a positive return on ETH where Buy~\&~Hold lost~$7\%$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Three-Stage Architecture}
Let $x_t \in \mathbb{R}^d$ denote the technical-indicator feature vector at day $t$. Stage~1 outputs a posterior
$\hat p_{\text{trend}}(t) = (\hat p_{\text{down}}, \hat p_{\text{range}}, \hat p_{\text{up}})$;
Stage~2 emits a hard one-hot $\hat p_{\text{regime}}(t) \in \{e_{\text{Bull}}, e_{\text{Neutral}}, e_{\text{Bear}}\}$ together with a regime-tenure scalar $\tau_t$;
Stage~3 receives the concatenated vector
\begin{equation}
z_t = \big[\hat p_{\text{trend}}(t),\ \hat p_{\text{trend}}^{(\text{smooth-10})}(t),\ \hat p_{\text{regime}}(t),\ \tau_t,\ o_t\big] \in \mathbb{R}^{16},
\end{equation}
where $o_t$ is the six-dimensional oscillator vector. The Stage-3 decision is
\begin{equation}
\hat y_t = \arg\max_{y \in \{\text{Buy}, \text{Hold}, \text{Sell}\}} \hat p(y \mid z_t).
\end{equation}

The signal label is generated from a causal adaptive threshold,
\begin{equation}
y_t \;=\;
\begin{cases}
\text{Buy} & \text{if } \tfrac{P_{t+5} - P_t}{P_t} > +k\,\hat\sigma_t,\\[2pt]
\text{Sell} & \text{if } \tfrac{P_{t+5} - P_t}{P_t} < -k\,\hat\sigma_t,\\[2pt]
\text{Hold} & \text{otherwise,}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:signal_label}
\end{equation}
where $\hat\sigma_t$ is the past-only rolling 20-day standard deviation of daily returns and $k=0.5$ (sensitivity reported in Section~\ref{sec:k_ablation}).

\subsection{Stage 2: Composite Macro Finite-State Machine}
\label{sec:fsm}
We adopted a deterministic finite-state machine (FSM) for Stage~2 \emph{only} after four canonical unsupervised approaches failed: vanilla K-Means could not capture the 2008 Global Financial Crisis structure; semantic-constrained K-Means produced noisy regime transitions; an HMM with three Gaussian states displayed unstable transitions; a 3-component GMM exhibited 2024--2025 stickiness with $P(\text{Stress})$ saturated near $1.0$. The retained FSM is governed by eight rules and one guard:
\textbf{(R1)}~VIX hysteresis (Bear entry $z>1.0$, exit $z<0.3$; symmetric Bull);
\textbf{(R2)}~minimum dwell (Bear $\geq 20$\,d, Bull $\geq 40$\,d, Neutral $\geq 10$\,d);
\textbf{(R3)}~Bear$\to$Neutral velocity ($\Delta\text{VIX}_z[10\text{d}] < -0.8$);
\textbf{(R4)}~persistent yield-curve inversion ($60$-day rolling US10Y\,$-$\,US2Y $<0$);
\textbf{(R5)}~DXY+M2 macro-stress override (DXY\,$z[30]>0.7$ AND M2\,YoY\,$<4\%$);
\textbf{(R6)}~Bull velocity entry (V-shape recovery);
\textbf{(R7)}~Bear velocity entry (rapid escalation);
\textbf{(R8)}~Bear re-entry guard ($35$-day Neutral dwell required after Bear$\to$Neutral). The resulting timeline (Fig.~\ref{fig:stage2}) correctly identifies the 2008 GFC, 2018-Q4, 2020-COVID, 2022 Q2--Q4, and 2025-04 tariff Bear regimes, plus the 2009, 2020-V, 2022-Q4, and 2025-05 V-shape recoveries.

\subsection{Stage 1: ZigZag-Labelled Trend Classifier}
The Stage-1 label is produced offline from price by a ZigZag pivot detector with $10\%$ deviation threshold and $\geq 15$-day minimum segment length, classifying the segment between two pivots as \texttt{uptrend} when the realised amplitude exceeds $7.5\%$ and the segment exceeds the minimum dwell, \texttt{downtrend} symmetrically, and \texttt{range} otherwise. Although the label is retrospectively defined, it is \emph{never} visible to the Stage-1 model at inference time: the model receives only causal technical indicators (RSI, MACD, ADX, Bollinger \%B, ATR, volume z-scores, OBV, six rolling-window log returns)---fourteen features in total.

\paragraph*{Why an ML classifier is needed despite an offline rule}
A pure rule cannot be deployed at inference time because the ZigZag label is itself \emph{revisable} once new price data arrives. We tested this empirically by recomputing the most recent ZigZag label at $106$ monthly cutoffs from 2017--2025 using \emph{only} the data available up to each cutoff and comparing it to the offline label produced from the full 2014--2025 dataset (Fig.~\ref{fig:zzlookahead}). The two disagreed in $44/106 \approx 42\%$ of cutoff dates. An ML model receiving frozen weights and $t$-only features yields a stable, causal posterior that does not change retroactively when new data arrives.

\subsection{Classifiers}
\label{sec:classifiers}
Four classifiers are evaluated at Stages 1 and 3.
\textbf{XGBoost}~\cite{chen2016xgboost} with \texttt{multi:softprob} objective and \texttt{tree\_method=hist}; inverse-frequency \texttt{sample\_weight} replaces \texttt{class\_weight=balanced}.
\textbf{LightGBM}~\cite{ke2017lightgbm} with \texttt{class\_weight=balanced} via the scikit-learn wrapper.
\textbf{Random Forest}~\cite{breiman2001rf} with \texttt{class\_weight=balanced}.
\textbf{MLP}~\cite{rumelhart1986} (two- or three-layer ReLU with early stopping); scikit-learn's MLP does not natively support \texttt{class\_weight} and we did not implement an oversampling fallback (paper limitation). Tree models receive raw features; MLP receives features standardised on the training fold only.

\paragraph*{Model assumptions} The three tree-based ensembles (XGBoost, LightGBM, Random Forest) are non-parametric: they make no assumption of class-conditional Gaussianity, equal covariance, or feature linearity, and they are scale-invariant, so no feature standardisation is required. They do, however, assume that training and test samples are drawn from the same distribution---an assumption known to be violated in a non-stationary regime-switching domain such as cryptocurrency, which is precisely why we use expanding-window walk-forward CV that re-trains at every fold to track regime drift. The MLP assumes feature scales of comparable order (we standardise per training fold) and a piecewise-linear decision surface induced by ReLU activations; it makes no Gaussianity assumption but is sensitive to local optima and learning-rate selection, addressed via early stopping and Optuna-tuned learning rate. None of the four models assume independent observations; the temporal correlation in daily price series is handled at the CV level (gap of $g=10$~days $>$ label horizon $h=5$~days) rather than at the model level.

The training procedure for each (asset, stage, model) configuration is: (i)~determine hyperparameters by Optuna on the inner-CV walk-forward; (ii)~fit the final model on each outer-fold training window with the selected hyperparameters; (iii)~predict the validation window producing OOF posteriors; (iv)~concatenate the per-fold OOF predictions to produce a single time-aligned OOF series spanning the full test horizon.

\subsection{Walk-Forward Cross-Validation and Hyperparameter Tuning}
We use \emph{expanding-window} walk-forward at the outer level: training set $[t_0, t_k]$, gap $g=10$~days (larger than the 5-day forward-return horizon to prevent label leakage), validation $[t_k+g,\, t_k+g+v]$ with $v=200$~days, advancing by $v$ (non-overlapping). This yields $16$ outer folds for BTC, $10$ for ETH at Stage~1, and $12/6$ folds at Stage~3. For hyperparameter tuning we use Optuna's TPE sampler with the MedianPruner and an \emph{inner} walk-forward of $5$ folds, evenly spaced across the dataset (\texttt{train\_min}=750, \texttt{val}=300, \texttt{gap}=10). The inner-fold dates were chosen to span distinct historical regimes (2017, 2019, 2021, 2023, 2025) so that the inner search would not overfit to a single market period.\footnote{An earlier 3-fold inner configuration tended to select degenerate hyperparameters (e.g.~\texttt{min\_samples\_leaf=1} for Random Forest) that did not generalise on the outer folds; the 5-fold inner CV recovered the generalisation gap.} Hyperparameter search spaces (30 trials per model and stage) are summarised in the supplementary code repository.

\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\textbf{Classification:} accuracy, precision (per class and macro-averaged), recall (per class and macro-averaged), F1 (per class and macro-averaged), multiclass one-vs-rest ROC-AUC (per class and macro-averaged), and confusion matrices, all computed on the concatenated outer-fold OOF predictions. \textbf{Segment-level} (Stage~1 only): majority-vote consistency, mean intersection-over-union, onset-detection F1 with $\pm 5$-day tolerance. \textbf{Economic} (Stage~3 only): annualised Sharpe ratio (252 trading days), total return, maximum drawdown, number of trades, win rate. Buy~\&~Hold benchmark on the same OOF span. Three trading rules---stateful long-only, defensive reset, probability-weighted---are evaluated on identical OOF predictions. Backtests assume a $0.1\%$ one-way transaction cost; slippage is not modelled (limitation).

\subsection{Software and Hardware}
Python 3.11; scikit-learn 1.8.0; XGBoost 3.2.0; LightGBM 4.6.0; Optuna 4.8.0. Experiments executed on a single 2024 MacBook Air (M3, 16~GB RAM). The full overnight ablation (4 phases, 4 architectures $\times$ 4 models $\times$ 2 assets $\times$ 30 Optuna trials with 5-fold inner CV plus retraining and backtests) completed in $1$h\,$43$m.

\subsection{Trading-Rule Ablation}
The stateful long-only rule (Buy = enter, Sell = exit, Hold = preserve state) maximises BTC Sharpe but entails the highest drawdown. The probability-weighted rule (continuous position size $\propto \mathrm{clip}(P_{\text{Buy}} - P_{\text{Sell}}, 0, 1)$) wins on ETH: it cuts drawdown to $18\%$ while delivering positive return where Buy~\&~Hold loses money. The defensive rule (Buy = long, otherwise cash) sits between the two with the highest trade count and the highest transaction-cost drag. A per-asset best rule emerges: BTC favours \emph{stateful} (the test period is bull-heavy); ETH favours \emph{probability-weighted} (volatile ETH requires soft sizing for risk management).

\subsection{Signal-Label Threshold Sensitivity}
\label{sec:k_ablation}
The threshold multiplier $k$ in (\ref{eq:signal_label}) controls the Buy/Hold/Sell balance. We tested $k \in \{0.4, 0.5, 0.7, 1.0\}$. For BTC, $k=0.5$ was optimal across all four classifiers. For ETH, $k=0.5$ was optimal for tree-based models, but the MLP's macro-F1 rose from $0.312$ ($k{=}0.5$) to $0.361$ ($k{=}1.0$). We retained $k=0.5$ throughout for fair cross-classifier comparison.

%% ===================== 5. DISCUSSION =====================
\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Frame-Level vs.~Trade-Level Performance}
The Stage-3 macro-F1 of $0.37$ sits below our pre-registered target of $0.40$ and only marginally above the chance baseline of $0.33$; ROC-AUC sits at $0.53$. Yet the same model, evaluated as a trading strategy, achieves Sharpe $1.15$ on BTC. We see two reasons. First, the Hold class operates as a \emph{risk-management filter}: stateful and probability-weighted rules use the Hold posterior to avoid trades on uncertain days, preserving capital that a 2-class classifier would have committed. This converts low frame accuracy into high economic precision. Second, a Sharpe of $1.15$ implies an average daily return-to-volatility ratio of $\sim 0.07$, corresponding to a per-day directional accuracy slightly above $50\%$---fully consistent with AUC $0.53$. The non-linearity arises from compounding and from selectivity ($179$ BTC trades vs.~$2{,}200$ trading days).

\subsection{The Hierarchical Architecture Is Not Universally Beneficial}
Our most striking finding is the asset-specific architecture optimum: 3-Stage Full is best for BTC, flat is best for ETH. We hypothesise that BTC's 10-year span captures multiple distinct regimes (2014--2017 nascent, 2018 bear, 2019--2020 recovery, 2021 bull peak, 2022 winter, 2023--2025 modern) that benefit from regime-aware features, while the ETH 5-year span is dominated by 2020--2022 dynamics in which raw oscillators carry sufficient information without upstream-posterior overhead. The practical implication is that \emph{every} hierarchical-architecture proposal in financial ML should be re-validated per asset and per dataset size; our four-architecture ablation provides a template.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}
(i)~Transaction-cost realism: $0.1\%$ one-way is conservative for liquid spot crypto but ignores spread, slippage and execution latency. (ii)~MLP class balancing was not implemented (no \texttt{class\_weight} in scikit-learn). (iii)~OOF span asymmetry: BTC starts in 2016 vs.~ETH 2020, partly explaining the architecture asymmetry. (iv)~No statistical-significance test (e.g.~McNemar~\cite{dietterich1998statistical}) over the OOF predictions. Future work will add Stage-3 posterior calibration, dynamic Kelly-style position sizing, ablation on the Stage-2 FSM rule set, and a re-tuned Stage~1 with the dev-$0.07$ ZigZag configuration identified by our distribution sweep.

%% ===================== 6. CONCLUSION =====================
\section{Conclusion}
We presented a three-stage hierarchical soft-fusion framework for cryptocurrency trading-signal classification, evaluated on BTC and ETH daily data with walk-forward cross-validation, Optuna hyperparameter tuning, four-classifier comparisons, and a four-architecture ablation. The framework achieves an annualised Sharpe of $1.15$ on BTC and a clean Sharpe-plus-return win on ETH versus Buy~\&~Hold---particularly striking given that ETH's Buy~\&~Hold lost $7\%$ over the test span while our model returned $+26\%$. Two findings stand out. First, \emph{architectural depth is asset-specific}: the full hierarchy helps BTC but overfits ETH, where a flat 6-feature baseline wins. Second, \emph{frame-level metrics underestimate trade-level value}: a macro-F1 of $0.37$ and AUC of $0.53$ nonetheless yield Sharpe $1.15$ once a Hold-aware trading rule is applied. The pipeline, code, and OOF predictions are reproducible and packaged in a single Docker container with a FastAPI back-end and Chart.js front-end for the live demonstration required by the course.
